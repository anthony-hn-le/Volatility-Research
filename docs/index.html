Statistical Accuracy vs. Economic Value in Equity Volatility Forecasting

Anthony Le, Class of 2027 Supervisors: Emily Marshall & Tyler Wake 18 assets · 9 models · 4 portfolio strategies · 2010–2025

Motivation & Research Questions

Machine learning keeps reopening Hansen & Lunde's (2005) question — "does anything beat a GARCH(1,1)?" — but most horse races stop at a loss function. This project asks three questions of nine volatility forecasters at once:

  • RQ1 — Accuracy. Do ML/hybrid models significantly beat econometric benchmarks, once corrected for testing nine models at once?
  • RQ2 — Economic value. Does that accuracy translate into better risk-adjusted portfolio returns, net of costs?
  • RQ3 — Resilience. Does any edge survive across regimes, sub-periods, and cost/leverage assumptions?

Data & Design

18 U.S. equities (3 indices + 15 large-caps), daily 2010–2025. Expanding-window walk-forward: trained through Dec 2017, 96 monthly refits tested Jan 2018–Dec 2025, forecasting 1/5/20-day realized volatility (21-day rolling std of log returns, annualized).

Econometric GARCH(1,1) · EGARCH(1,1) · GJR-GARCH(1,1) · HAR-RV

Machine learning Random Forest · XGBoost · LSTM

Hybrid GARCH-RF · GARCH-XGBoost

Random Forest is the only model to survive a 90%-confidence Model Confidence Set on pooled 1-day squared-error loss across all 18 assets — and it isn't a mean-driven fluke: it has the single lowest RMSE on every individual asset in the universe.

But the edge is short-horizon only: RF's RMSE at the 20-day horizon (14.3) is worse than GARCH(1,1)'s (12.2) — it reuses its 1-day prediction at every horizon, while GARCH forecasts a genuine multi-step path.

Cost vs. Benefit

~24 hrs
LSTM walk-forward compute

LSTM's 86,864 seconds (~24 hours) of walk-forward compute buys a +0.68 Sharpe gain over GARCH(1,1) in its best strategy (VRP). GJR-GARCH(1,1) — fit in under a minute — buys +0.83, the best economic gain of any model in the study. Measured per unit of compute, the cheapest model wins.

Robustness Checks

  • Cross-asset: RF's edge holds on 18/18 assets, not a mean artifact.
  • Cost/leverage sensitivity: GARCH(1,1)'s Sharpe is flat across 1–3x leverage; RF is leverage-sensitive (0.50 → 0.29).
  • Sub-period stability: zero Sharpe sign flips, 9 models × 2 strategies × pre/post-2022 halves.

Conclusion

Random Forest wins on accuracy — broadly, significantly, at short horizons. It never wins on economic value, because the strategies that most need accuracy need it at a horizon where RF's edge disappears. Where portfolios do benefit from a good forecast, an inexpensive econometric model captures the gain. Match model complexity to the horizon and mechanism in use — don't default to the most sophisticated model available.
